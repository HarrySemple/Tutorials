    Section The Normal Distribution I

    Imagine you are throwing darts onto the \(\mathbb{R}^2\)-plane, aiming for the origin \((0,0)\text{.}\) After hundereds of darts, what does the plane look like?
    Assuming you’re not a perfect shot, we would expect some variation - a dense cluster at the centre that thins out further away from the origin. We also expect the scattering to be radially symmetric.
    Let \(dA\subset\mathbb{R}^2\) be a small region in the plane. We can model the probability of a dart landing in \(dA\) as
    \begin{align} \mathbb{P}(\text{dart lands in }dA) =\amp \phi(r)\lambda_2 (dA)\tag{1} \end{align}
    where \(\lambda_2(dA)\) is the area of \(dA\) and \(\phi\) is some monotone decreasing funtion \(\mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}\rightarrow\mathbb{R}_{>0}\text{,}\) with \(r = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}\) the distance away from the origin of a token point \((x,y)\in dA\text{.}\) Note that \(\theta\) doesn’t appear in our model due to radial symmetry.

    Is it measurable?

    \(\lambda_2\) is known as the 2D Lebesgue measure. Precisely defining this function has to be done with extreme care in order to avoid paradoxes caused by certain difficult (non-measurable) subsets of \(\mathbb{R}^2\text{.}\) More detail can be found here
     6 
    people.bath.ac.uk/masmdp/measdir.bho/notesp1to10.pdf
    .
    For a single dart, the \(x\) and \(y\) coordinates are independent, allowing us to introduce
    \begin{equation*} \mathbb{P}(\text{dart lands in }dA) = \phi(r)\lambda_2 (dA) = f(x)f(y)\lambda_2 (dA) \end{equation*}
    where \(f:\mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}\rightarrow\mathbb{R}_{>0}\) is another monotone decreasing function, with the relation
    \begin{align*} \phi(\sqrt{x^2+y^2}) =\amp f(x)f(y). \end{align*}
    Setting \(y=0\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{align*} \phi(x) =\amp f(x)f(0) = \psi f(x) \end{align*}
    where \(\psi = f(0) > 0\) is some constant. Thus,
    \begin{align*} \phi(\sqrt{x^2+y^2}) =\amp \psi f(\sqrt{x^2+y^2}) =f(x)f(y) \end{align*}
    dividing through by \(\psi^2\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{align*} \frac{1}{\psi}f(\sqrt{x^2+y^2}) =\amp \frac{1}{\psi}f(x)\frac{1}{\psi}f(y). \end{align*}
    Define \(g:\mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}\rightarrow\mathbb{R}_{>0}\) such that \(g(x) = \frac{1}{\psi}f(x)\text{.}\) We have that \(g\) satisfies
    \begin{equation*} g(\sqrt{x^2+y^2}) = g(x)g(y). \end{equation*}
    Do we know what type of function \(g\) is likely to be? Can you think of an example?

    Exercises Exercises

    Ex 28.

    Find a function \(h(x)\) that satisfies
    \begin{equation} h(x+y) = h(x)h(y).\tag{2} \end{equation}
    What is the general form of functions that satisfy (2)?
    Answer.
    \begin{equation*} h(x) = e^{Ax} \end{equation*}

    Ex 29.

    Find a function \(g(x)\) that satisfies
    \begin{equation} g(\sqrt{x^2+y^2}) = g(x)g(y).\tag{3} \end{equation}
    What is the general form of functions that satisfy (3)?
    Hint.
    Stay with exponentials!
    Answer.
    \begin{equation*} g(x) = e^{Ax^2} \end{equation*}
